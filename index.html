Calculator Overview & Goal ❯

This tool is an interactive calculator designed to help explore an advanced estate planning strategy known as an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT).

Goal: The primary goal is to estimate and compare the net lifetime benefits of gifting or selling any individual asset to an IDGT. It tries to identify which assets might be more "efficient" for this strategy by looking at projected tax savings, overall wealth transfer to beneficiaries, and an "Efficiency Ratio." The calculator also attempts to find the "Optimal Swap Timing" – the best projected year to potentially exchange an asset out of the IDGT to take advantage of a step-up in basis at death.

Important Disclaimer: This is a simplified model for educational and illustrative purposes only. It is not financial, tax, or legal advice. Always consult with qualified professionals.

    Understanding Optimal Swap Timing ❯

    The 'Optimal Swap Timing' is the calculated future year when it's projected to be most financially advantageous to use the IDGT's 'swap power.' This means bringing the specific asset out of the IDGT and back into your personal estate (typically in exchange for cash or other assets of equal value, now explicitly modeled with its own return/tax profile).

    How it's determined (with refined logic):

    • For each asset, the calculator simulates a 'swap' for every possible future year.
    • For each simulated swap year, it recalculates the total expected financial benefit (Net Present Value or NPV). This NPV considers:
      • Tax Burn Benefit: Value of you paying the IDGT's income taxes. Before the swap, this is on the original asset's yield and its post-contribution capital gains (if sold by IDGT). At and after the swap, this is on the *newly swapped-in asset's* income/gains.
      • Estate Tax Impact: This is a net calculation. If death is *before* the swap, the original asset is out of your estate (saving tax). If death is *at or after* the swap:
        • The original asset is back in your estate (costing estate tax on its value).
        • The *newly swapped-in asset* is now out of your estate (saving estate tax on its value).
        • Refinement: The value of the original asset passing at death (after being swapped out) is also considered to have *forfeited* the "tax-exclusive gift benefit" (a Value * Rate2 type saving) it would have achieved if an equivalent value had been gifted during life. This forfeited benefit reduces the NPV of the swap scenario.
      • Step-Up in Basis Impact: If death is *before* the swap, the original asset (in IDGT) doesn't get a step-up (cost to heirs). If death is *at or after* the swap, the original asset (back in your estate) *does* get a step-up (benefit to heirs). The *newly swapped-in asset* (in IDGT) would have its own basis considerations for heirs.
    • All outcomes are weighted by the probability of passing away in each year and discounted to today's dollars.
    • The 'Optimal Swap Year' yields the highest NPV for that asset across all these possibilities.
    • An early optimal swap (e.g., Year 1) might occur if the original asset has a very large embedded gain (making immediate step-up highly valuable, even with the new forfeited benefit adjustment), low ongoing tax burn potential, and/or if the discount rate heavily favors near-term benefits.
    Key Calculation Note: Tax Burn on Capital Gains ❯

    For the purpose of calculating the Net Present Value (NPV) and the Efficiency Ratio in this tool, the "tax burn" benefit derived from an asset sale by the IDGT is specifically defined:

    • The calculator counts the tax paid by you (the grantor) on capital gains that accrue after the asset was contributed to the IDGT as a positive component of the "PV Tax Burn." This reflects the "alpha" or additional wealth generated and tax-shielded due to the IDGT structure on new growth.
    • If an asset has pre-existing (embedded) capital gains at the time it's gifted, and the IDGT later sells this asset, you (the grantor) are still legally responsible for paying the capital gains tax on that pre-existing portion (calculated from the original carryover basis). This payment genuinely reduces your personal estate (which is beneficial for overall estate tax purposes) and allows the IDGT to retain the full gross sale proceeds.
    • However, this calculator, for its specific "PV Tax Burn" component contributing to the NPV and Efficiency Ratio, isolates the benefit to post-contribution gains. This approach aims to provide a clearer comparison of assets based on the new value and tax efficiency generated after they are placed into the IDGT structure for its growth phase, rather than giving "alpha" credit in the tax burn line item for taxes on gains that existed prior to the gift.
    • The overall estate tax savings calculation (PV Estate Tax Impact) still considers the removal of the asset's full fair market value (including pre-existing gains and all future growth until a potential swap) from your estate, and is now more accurately assessed based on the `Total Grantor Estate` input.
    General Notes & Limitations ❯
    • This calculator is for illustrative and educational purposes only. It is not financial, tax, or legal advice.
    • The accuracy depends heavily on input accuracy and underlying assumptions. Tax laws and mortality data can change.
    • Mortality data used is from the SSA 2021 Period Life Table. Professional analysis may use different tables or adjustments.
    • Federal estate tax exemption is inflated annually; however, detailed income tax bracket inflation is not modeled for the grantor's income tax rates. Tax rates are assumed to be static.
    • The estate tax calculation now considers the `Total Grantor Estate` input for a more realistic assessment of whether the estate (including IDGT assets if swapped back or never gifted) would exceed the projected exemption. However, it still simplifies certain complex interactions and does not model all possible estate planning nuances.
    • State-specific tax nuances (e.g., deductibility of state taxes for federal income tax) are simplified. State estate/inheritance taxes are not modeled here.
    • Always consult with qualified financial advisors, estate planning attorneys, and tax professionals for personalized advice.
